kudo millet

Here are some details about kodo millet: Appearance: Kodo millet is an annual grass that grows to about four feet tall. It has light green leaves that are 20–40 centimeters long and small, ellipsoidal seeds that are 1.5 mm wide and 2 mm long. The seeds can be light brown to dark gray in color. Nutrition: Kodo millet is a good source of vitamins, minerals, carbohydrates, phytochemicals, essential amino acids, antioxidants, and lecithin. It's also high in dietary fiber and iron. pearl millet

Appearance Pearl millet is a warm-season annual grass crop with ovoid grains that can be white, pale yellow, brown, grey, slate blue, or purple. The grains are 3–4 millimeters long, making them the largest kernels of all millet varieties. Uses: Pearl millet is primarily known in the U.S. as a forage crop, but new varieties are being developed for use as a grain crop. Pearl millet can also be used as a cover crop, where research suggests it can reduce root lesion nematodes. Nutrition Pearl millet is high in protein, fiber, and B vitamins, and also contains calcium, magnesium, iron, and zinc. It has a similar amount of digestible energy to corn, but a higher protein percentage.

Foxtail millet

Foxtail millet (Setaria italica) is an annual grass that is native to southern Asia and is one of the oldest cultivated crops: Uses Foxtail millet is grown in arid and semi-arid regions of Asia and Africa, and is used for food, bird feed, hay, and silage. It can also be used for erosion control and afforestation. Nutrition Foxtail millet is gluten-free, high in protein, and low in carbohydrates. It contains many minerals, including phosphorous, calcium, iron, zinc, magnesium, and sodium. Growing conditions

Little millet

Little millet, also known as Panicum sumatrense, is a small millet grain with many nutritional benefits and uses: Origin Little millet is native to India and was first domesticated there. It was discovered at the Indus Valley Civilization site of Harappa around 2600 BC. Nutrition Little millet is a good source of protein, fiber, calcium, iron, and vitamins B and E. It's also rich in antioxidants and phytochemicals like phenolic acids, flavonoids, tannins, and phytate. Gluten-free: Little millet is gluten-free, making it a good option for people with gluten intolerance.
